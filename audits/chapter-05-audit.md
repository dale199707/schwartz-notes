| Q# | Verification question | Source-grounded answer | Chapter finding before revision | Verdict before revision | Correction | Sources |
|---:|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Does elevated lactate prove anaerobic shock metabolism? | No. Hypoperfusion is important, but adrenergic aerobic glycolysis, impaired clearance, drugs, and other causes also elevate lactate. | Described lactate as a direct consequence of universal anaerobic conversion. | NEEDS_NUANCE | Added nonhypoxic causes and noted that normal lactate cannot exclude shock. | [SSC 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34599691/) |
| 2 | Is compensated shock possible before hypotension? | Yes; tachycardia, altered perfusion, mental status, or metabolic abnormalities can precede low blood pressure. | Correct. | SUPPORTED | Retained. | Schwartz 11e |
| 3 | Is definitive relief of obstruction central in obstructive shock? | Yes; tension pneumothorax, tamponade, and massive PE require cause-specific relief, not pressors alone. | Correct. | SUPPORTED | None. | Schwartz 11e |
| 4 | Can cardiogenic shock be managed as “inotrope/MCS and avoid fluids”? | No. Identify phenotype and reversible cause; hypotensive patients often need norepinephrine, inotropes and MCS are selective, and RV/preload states differ. | Overgeneralized hemodynamics and treatment. | NEEDS_NUANCE | Added echo/hemodynamic phenotyping, revascularization/causal treatment, selective drugs and MCS. | [ACC 2025](https://www.acc.org/guidelines/guidelines/2025/03/17/19/42/cardiogenic-shock-concise-clinical-guidance) |
| 5 | Must early septic shock be warm and high-output? | No; cardiac output may be high, normal, or low and can change over time. | Used high-output warm shock as the expected early pattern. | NEEDS_NUANCE | Reframed as a common but nonessential phenotype. | [SSC 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34599691/) |
| 6 | May antihistamines or steroids delay epinephrine in anaphylaxis? | No. IM epinephrine is first-line; antihistamines help selected symptoms and steroids do not reliably prevent biphasic reactions. | Called them adjuncts without explicitly preventing delay or correcting the biphasic misconception. | NEEDS_NUANCE | Added their limited roles and first-line priority. | [WAO 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/33204386/), [Practice parameter](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32001253/) |
| 7 | Is “lactate clearance” a sufficient resuscitation endpoint? | No. Use serial lactate with capillary refill, urine output, mental status, ultrasound/hemodynamics, and clinical trajectory. | Listed several endpoints but foregrounded lactate clearance without its limitations. | NEEDS_NUANCE | Replaced single-marker language with multimodal reassessment. | [SSC 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34599691/) |
| 8 | Is norepinephrine first-line in septic shock and vasopressin a common adjunct? | Yes; use dynamic measures for further fluids and titrate to perfusion, not a fixed drug-only algorithm. | Correct. | SUPPORTED | Retained and sharpened. | [SSC 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34599691/) |
| 9 | Is permissive hypotension inappropriate in TBI? | Yes; avoid secondary cerebral ischemia from hypotension and hypoxia. | Correct. | SUPPORTED | None. | [European trauma guideline](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36859355/) |
| 10 | Did ATHOS-3 demonstrate a mortality benefit from angiotensin II? | No; it improved blood-pressure response in refractory vasodilatory shock, with no established mortality benefit. | Correctly limited the claim to pressure response and noted thrombosis. | SUPPORTED | Retained. | [ATHOS-3](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28528561/) |

## Re-audit conclusion

After revision, all ten questions are `SUPPORTED`. The chapter now avoids treating shock phenotypes, lactate, or vasoactive drugs as universal one-variable rules.

Cardiogenic shock device selection remains evidence-limited and institution dependent. A 10/10 audit score is a quality threshold, not a guarantee or a substitute for bedside specialist input.
